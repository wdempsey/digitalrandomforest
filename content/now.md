## June 2026

The question I'm actually working on: can AI assistants become real infrastructure for academic data science — not chatbots that hand back answers, but working systems for research planning, coding, writing, judgment, and project memory?

That reframes the day-to-day tool comparison (Codex, Claude, and others) as a means rather than the point. The test isn't which model is more impressive in a single exchange. It's which configuration of tools, workflows, and habits actually compounds judgment across a project — the [[garden/essays/why-data-sciences-future-is-an-ecosystem-story|ecosystem argument]] applied to my own desk.

Current focus:

- treating AI tools as a research environment, not a Q&A box: planning, scoping, code, drafting, and project memory
- comparing where each tool earns its place — repo work and workflow design vs. conceptual drafting, long-context synthesis, and critique
- building the small infrastructure that should survive a model swap: evaluation notes, workflow traces, reusable patterns
- writing the result up as notes on AI-assisted research judgment

Open questions:

1. What belongs to the *system* (reusable, logged, inspectable) versus the *model* (rented, interchangeable)?
2. Where does each tool fit best without losing the thread of judgment across them?
3. Which parts of research thinking must stay inside the loop rather than collapse into end-point verification?
4. What does "project memory" concretely look like for a research group — and what makes it survive a departing student?
