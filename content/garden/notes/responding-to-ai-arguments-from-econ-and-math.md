This note is a holding space for responding to AI arguments that come primarily from economics and mathematics.

The goal is not to flatten those arguments into a single pro- or anti-AI position.

It is to ask a narrower question:

What do these arguments get right, what do they miss about real data science practice, and where do they become more persuasive once we think in terms of workflows, institutions, and judgment?

## Why this should be a note first

This topic will probably accumulate citations, contrasts, and partial disagreements before it becomes a stable argument.

That makes it a good `note` rather than an essay for now.

The note can collect:

- claims from economists about incentives, productivity, labor, and substitution
- claims from mathematicians about rigor, abstraction, generality, and proof-like standards
- responses from the point of view of applied statistics and data science practice

## Questions to return to

- Which econ arguments are really about institutions rather than models?
- Which math arguments assume a cleaner problem than applied work usually provides?
- Where do both traditions underestimate workflow design and human judgment?
- Which arguments should grow into standalone essays later?
