The seed this note planted has grown into an essay. For the developed argument, see [[garden/essays/why-data-sciences-future-is-an-ecosystem-story|Why Data Science's Future Is an Ecosystem Story]]. This note keeps the original spark.

A frontier model alone is not a stable end state. The deeper opportunity is a frontier *ecosystem*: tools, workflows, evaluation loops, and organizational memory that let people build on top of model capabilities rather than rent access to them.

That idea rhymes with David Donoho's argument in [[https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.00865|Data Science at the Singularity]]: much of modern data science progress came from open, low-friction exchange — shared data, shared code, public challenges — not from a single magical breakthrough. R and Python were not just languages; they became coordination systems that let good ideas spread and weak ones fail in the open.

So the strategic question is not only *which model is best right now?* It is *what ecosystem lets a team preserve judgment, reuse workflows, and keep compounding knowledge even as base models change?* The open question I started from still holds: in the AI era, what is the data science equivalent of building on Linux instead of buying an appliance?
