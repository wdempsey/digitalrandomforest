Academics may be better prepared for AI-assisted work than the usual stereotypes suggest.

One reason is that a lot of academic work is already managerial.

Researchers routinely scope ambiguous projects, break vague goals into tractable tasks, define what counts as evidence, delegate pieces of work, review partial outputs, and integrate imperfect results into a coherent argument.

That is close to the core skill of working well with AI systems.

The main task is not only to ask a model for an answer.

It is to define the work well enough that the model can contribute usefully, then keep enough judgment in the loop to decide whether the contribution actually advances the project.

## Working claim

AI delegation rewards the same skills that make a good research manager:

- defining the question
- decomposing the work
- specifying constraints
- identifying failure modes
- reviewing intermediate outputs
- knowing when a task is underspecified

This suggests that academics should not think of AI primarily as a replacement for expertise.

They should think of it as a delegation environment where project scoping becomes more valuable.

## Open question

What parts of academic training already teach AI delegation, and which parts need to be made explicit?
