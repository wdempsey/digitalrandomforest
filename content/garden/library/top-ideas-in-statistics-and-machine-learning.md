This page is a sequence hub for short stat notes on major ideas in statistics, followed by a comparable map of machine learning ideas.

The first anchors are Roderick Little's work on seminal ideas and controversies in statistics and the Stanford Statistics 319 journal club on statistical ideas that changed the world.

From there, I want to augment the sequence with:

- Hastie and Tibshirani interviews
- canonical lists of major statistical ideas
- an eventual companion list of top ideas in machine learning

## Why this sequence matters

A lot of current AI conversation lacks historical depth about what statistics already solved, formalized, or worried about.

This sequence is a way to build that depth deliberately, one compact note at a time.

It should help answer:

- Which statistical ideas still organize good practice?
- Which machine learning ideas extend them?
- Where do the two traditions genuinely diverge?

## Planned path

1. Read and summarize Little's framing of seminal ideas and controversies.
2. Work through the Stanford journal club list as a sequence of short stat notes.
3. Compare with oral-history style reflections from Hastie and Tibshirani.
4. Build a provisional list of top ideas in machine learning.
5. Write short comparative notes on overlaps, omissions, and tensions.
