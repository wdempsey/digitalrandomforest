This page is a sequence hub for short stat notes on foundational concepts and applied examples from McCullagh, read with D. R. Cox in mind.

The aim is not just to summarize examples.

It is to surface what they teach about:

- model choice
- scientific question formulation
- interpretation
- simplification under real constraints

## Source trail

- [[https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-14275-8_11|Peter McCullagh, "Basic Concepts" in Ten Projects in Applied Statistics]]

## Why use a Cox lens

D. R. Cox is a useful guide because he consistently treated statistics as a discipline of careful reasoning about scientific questions, not just a collection of estimation procedures.

That makes him a strong counterweight to any account of data science that reduces practice to tooling speed.

## Planned structure

1. Start with McCullagh's foundational distinctions: probability, stochastic process, baseline, protocol, population, sample, and experimental unit.
2. Select ten applied examples from McCullagh.
3. Write one short note on each foundation or example.
4. Ask what Cox would want us to notice in the setup, assumptions, and interpretation.
5. Pull out recurring principles about applied statistical thinking.

## What this sequence should produce

- a set of short stat notes on examples
- a set of short foundational notes on concepts that organize applied work
- a recurring vocabulary for applied statistical judgment
- a teaching resource for students learning how examples carry ideas

## Open question

What makes an example genuinely instructive in statistics: technical novelty, inferential clarity, or the way it sharpens the scientific question?
