# Site Identity

## Purpose

This repository hosts a public digital garden built with Quartz.

The site is a place to connect ideas across:

- AI workflows for data scientists
- academic statistics
- causal inference
- software and methodological thinking
- literature notes
- evolving public research thoughts

The author is a builder of AI workflows for data scientists trying to understand and adapt to the fast-moving world of AI within academic statistics.

This site is not a product documentation site and not a personal diary.
It is a **research garden**: an evolving collection of ideas, notes, essays, and academic reflections.

---

# Design Philosophy

The aesthetic should follow the spirit of Maggie Appleton’s digital garden.

Not a clone — but the same feeling.

Target mood:

- quiet study
- warm
- reflective
- thoughtful
- editorial
- human
- calm

The site should feel like:

- a notebook kept over many years
- a place for thinking in public
- a slowly growing map of ideas

Avoid:

- developer documentation aesthetics
- dashboard-style interfaces
- dense UI or heavy sidebars
- loud colors or aggressive branding

---

# Visual Direction

Key characteristics:

- warm off-white / parchment background
- dark charcoal text (not pure black)
- serif headers
- readable sans-serif body text
- generous whitespace
- comfortable line length
- restrained color palette

Reading experience should always take priority over visual novelty.

Pages should feel like **essays or notes in a quiet study**, not technical documentation.

---

# Content Types

The garden contains several kinds of writing.

### Essays
Longer reflections or arguments.

Usually polished but still evolving.

### Notes
Shorter ideas, partial thoughts, or working explanations.

These form the connective tissue of the garden.

### Patterns
Reusable ideas about:

- AI workflows
- research workflows
- software practices
- thinking tools

These are often practical insights.

### Library
Academic literature notes and research summaries.

These are the most scholarly pieces on the site.

They may include:

- paper summaries
- methodological commentary
- links between academic ideas

---

# Content Tone

Writing should aim to be:

- precise
- reflective
- curious
- honest about uncertainty
- readable to technically literate audiences

Avoid hype or exaggerated claims.

Prefer clear explanations and careful reasoning.

---

# Mathematics and Code

Unlike many digital gardens, this site will regularly include:

- equations
- statistical notation
- code snippets
- algorithm explanations

Math and code should be styled elegantly so they feel native to the site rather than breaking the visual aesthetic.

---

# Sketches and Illustrations

Essays may include one minimal sketch or illustration.

These sketches should feel:

- light
- quiet
- exploratory
- notebook-like

They should not dominate the page.

If a sketch is present it should complement the writing rather than serve as decoration.

---

# Navigation Philosophy

Navigation should emphasize calm exploration.

Visitors should be able to:

- read essays and notes comfortably
- follow links between ideas
- explore topics gradually

Graph exploration and backlink features may exist but should remain **secondary** to the reading experience.

The homepage should be welcoming and editorial rather than autogenerated.

---

# Long-Term Vision

Over time the site should become:

- a map of ideas across AI, statistics, and research practice
- a place where essays grow out of notes
- a connected archive of thinking in public

The garden metaphor matters:

Ideas are planted, grow, connect, and occasionally become essays.

The system should support **slow accumulation of thought** rather than fast publishing.
